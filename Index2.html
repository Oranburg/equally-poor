Data Visualization — U.S. Economic History

Inequality & Poverty in America
1913–2024

Two measures of economic distribution — relative and absolute — plotted across eleven decades of American history. Income shares from federal tax returns; poverty from Census household surveys.
Sources: Piketty-Saez (Q.J. Econ. 2003, updated 2024) · U.S. Census Bureau CPS ASEC
Series
⚠ Methodological Note for Citation: Dashed poverty line (pre-1959) represents unofficial projections by Plotnick et al. (1998) and Fisher (1986) — not observed data. Series breaks in 1993, 2013, and 2017 affect Census Gini and poverty comparability. Piketty-Saez income shares reflect IRS fiscal income of tax units (pre-tax, pre-transfer); income-retiming around tax law changes creates visible spikes (notably 2012 and 1988). The Gini coefficient (optional series) is based on Census money income, which excludes capital gains and in-kind benefits. See Auten & Splinter, J. Pol. Econ. 132:7 (2024) for a competing lower estimate of inequality growth.
Primary Sources
Inequality: Thomas Piketty & Emmanuel Saez, Income Inequality in the United States, 1913–1998, 118 Q.J. Econ. 1 (2003) (updated Mar. 2024), eml.berkeley.edu/~saez/TabFig2022.xlsx. Gini: U.S. Census Bureau, CPS ASEC, Historical Income Tables Table A-4b, FRED GINIALLRH.
Poverty (1959–2024): Emily A. Shrider, Poverty in the United States: 2024, P60-287 (Sept. 2025), census.gov Historical Poverty Tables.
Pre-1959 estimates: Robert D. Plotnick et al., in Cambridge Economic History of the United States, Vol. 3, at 249 (2000); Gordon M. Fisher, ASPE/HHS (1986). Both series are unofficial projections; Fisher withdrew his estimates in 1999.
